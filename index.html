The genome promised us everything. It delivered half.

For decades, we've sequenced genomes and measured transcripts, believing RNA would tell us everything about cellular function. It doesn't.

Proteins are the true executors of life. They fold, they modify, they interact, they degrade—all beyond the reach of transcriptomics. GWAS studies identify thousands of disease-associated variants, yet most remain mechanistically unexplained.

Genoproteomics bridges this gap. We systematically map how every genetic variant shapes the proteome—its structure, modifications, interactions, and dynamics. Not predictions. Not proxies. Direct, measurable, actionable protein phenotypes.

Our Mission

To build the complete map from genetic variation to protein phenotype—enabling precision medicine that actually reaches the molecules that matter.

"Most drugs target proteins, not RNA. Most cellular functions are executed by proteins, not RNA. It's time our understanding of genetic variation caught up with this reality."

Flagship Project

The Variant-to-Proteotype Atlas

Mapping Missense Variants to Structural Consequences

Our inaugural project systematically maps how coding variants in disease-associated genes alter protein structure, stability, and interaction interfaces.

Using a combination of AlphaFold predictions, molecular dynamics simulations, and experimental validation through hydrogen-deuterium exchange mass spectrometry (HDX-MS), we're building the first comprehensive atlas linking genotype to structural proteotype.

Phase 1 targets 500 disease genes implicated in cancer, neurodegeneration, and rare genetic disorders—focusing on variants of uncertain significance (VUS) that current methods cannot interpret.

The Genoproteomics Framework

From Sequence to Structure to Function

1

Variant Identification

Curate disease-associated and population variants from GWAS, ClinVar, and gnomAD databases with protein-level annotations.

2

Structural Modeling

Predict variant impact on 3D structure using AlphaFold, molecular dynamics, and free energy calculations.

3

Experimental Validation

Validate structural predictions with HDX-MS, thermal stability assays, and protein-protein interaction mapping.

4

Mechanistic Integration

Build quantitative models linking variants to proteome phenotypes: abundance, PTMs, localization, and turnover.

Leadership

Kushal Raj Roy

A computational structural biologist pioneering the integration of genetic variation with protein structure and function. With expertise spanning molecular dynamics simulations, AlphaFold predictions, and mass spectrometry-based proteomics, Kushal is building the scientific foundation for genoproteomics.

His research focuses on translational GTPases and ribosomal translation mechanisms—the molecular machines that convert genetic information into proteins. This work revealed how subtle sequence variations propagate through protein structure to alter cellular function.

Genoproteomics emerged from a simple observation: we have the tools to connect every genetic variant to its protein-level consequences. We just need to do the work.

Specialty: Computational Structural Biology Focus: Translational GTPases Methods: MD, AlphaFold, MS
